# Visual DNA Framework (Art-Direction Layer)

> Distilled from the AI Video Creators course (BRO-1525) — see broomva/workspace docs/reference/ai-video-creators-course/.

This document specifies the art-direction and strategy layer that sits *above* asset generation. `brand-dna-extraction.md` answers "how does this brand look?" by measuring existing assets. This file answers the prior question: **"what should this brand mean and feel, before any pixel exists?"** — the decision layer that separates AI slop from work brands actually pay for.

The two complement each other. Visual DNA Framework decides the *intent* (strategy → positioning → concept → system); Brand DNA Extraction measures the *realized output* (color → composition → lighting → texture → pose). Run this framework first when shaping a new brand; run extraction when reverse-engineering an existing one.

> **Version note:** Model names in this file (Kling 3.0, Nano Banana Pro, Seedance 2.0, etc.) are accurate at time of distillation (late 2025 / early 2026) but **rotate quarterly** — the course itself says model leadership "rotates." Treat exact model names, credit costs, and free-tier claims as perishable. The *framework* is durable craft; the model list is not.

## 0. The Single Thesis

**Meaning → Associations → Trust → Conversion.**

Pretty visuals do not automatically build trust or sales. Visuals work only when they create the *right associations* in someone's mind — premium, reliable, calm, bold, precise. Those associations are **designed, not random**. Art direction is the decision-making layer *above* design: it decides the brand's visual language *before* fonts, colors, or templates are chosen.

> **"Clients don't pay for beauty. They pay for clarity, confidence, and reduced risk."**

The operational consequence is one rule that reorders everything:

> **Picking a font is the LAST decision, not the first.**

**Why this works:** the human brain reads associations faster than it reads content. A viewer decides "premium / cheap / trustworthy / amateur" in roughly one second — before processing a single word. If you choose fonts and colors first, you are decorating an undecided meaning. If you decide the meaning first, every later choice (font, color, spacing, imagery) becomes a *filter check* ("does this support the meaning?") instead of a guess. This is the difference between a defensible system and a pile of pretty assets that don't add up.

## 1. The Visual DNA Pyramid

Consistency is built at the **top** of the pyramid. Amateurs only touch layer 5.

| Layer | Name | What it is | Who works here |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Strategy** | Goal + Audience + Offer | Pros (first) |
| 2 | **Positioning** | What you want to be known for (one sentence) | Pros |
| 3 | **Concept** | One big idea / world | Pros |
| 4 | **System** | Repeatable rules (color roles, typography roles, layout/grid rhythm, imagery rules, graphic elements, proof presentation) | Pros |
| 5 | **Assets** | Covers, carousels, thumbnails, ads, portfolio | **Amateurs (only)** |

The pyramid **inverts amateur effort.** Amateurs spend 100% of their time at layer 5 (picking fonts, colors, shapes per-post) and wonder why their output is inconsistent. Pros spend the time at the top — strategy, positioning, concept, system — and then layer 5 becomes *fast and automatically consistent* because every asset inherits its rules from the system above it.

> "If you only work on layer 5, it's not *bad* — but you can't build *selling* branding that way. High-end branding requires taking care of the whole pyramid (which is why agencies charge much more for full branding than for 'quickly design me a cover')."

**Why this works:** consistency is not produced by making more posts — it is produced once, at the system layer, and then *inherited* by every asset. Each asset only has to obey rules that already exist. Effort spent at the top compounds across all future output; effort spent at the bottom must be re-spent on every single asset. This is also the **value ladder**: a "quick cover" is cheap because it touches only layer 5; "full branding" is the upsell because it builds the whole pyramid (reference point cited: a high-end website at a big agency = "$20K").

> **Time split observed:** ~3 hours building the legend/concept (layers 1–4), ~30 min getting results (layer 5). "Spend the time on the legend; everything after is fast."

## 2. Brand Core — 5 Lines

The Brand Core is your **filter for every visual choice.** Before any references, moodboards, or assets, write these five lines. They are the constitution every later decision is checked against.

| # | Line | Question it answers |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Audience** | Who exactly is it for? |
| 2 | **Transformation** | Before → after (the arc of your brand) |
| 3 | **Promise** | What you deliver consistently |
| 4 | **Belief** | What you stand for (values) |
| 5 | **Proof** | Why they should believe you — **and how you show it visually** |

**Why this works:** line 5 is the one amateurs skip and pros obsess over. "Proof" forces the brand to commit to *visual* credibility (portfolio format, case slides, before/after, numbers shown boldly) — not just textual claims. The other four lines keep every reference, moodboard lane, and asset honest: if a beautiful reference doesn't serve the Audience or support the Promise, it is *out*, no matter how good it looks.

### Brand-already-exists perception check

If the brand already exists, do not invent the core — *audit* it. Ask the audience these five questions and let the answers seed the core:

```text
1. "What 3 words do you associate with my brand?"
2. "What do you think I'm best at?"
3. "What do you trust me for / why would you trust me?"
4. "Why would you NOT trust me?"
5. "What feels confusing or inconsistent?"
```

The gap between the words you *want* (line 1–4 intent) and the words you *get* (audience answers) is the exact work the visual system has to do.

## 3. Concept Statement & Positioning One-Liner (verbatim templates)

These two templates compress the top three pyramid layers into sentences you can carry in your head and check work against. Use them verbatim — the fill-in slots are load-bearing.

### Concept Statement (one sentence)

```text
We help [audience] get [result] by [method], through [tone/experience].
```

### Positioning One-Liner (used live in the workshop)

```text
I help [audience] get [results] by/during [method], with a vibe of [word 1, word 2, word 3].
```

Example, verbatim from the instructor:

```text
I help brands create cinematic ads with commercial clarity, with a vibe of cinematic, precise and emotional.
```

**The 3 vibe words are the operational payload.** They become your filter for references and moodboard work. Keep them *specific*: not "cool" → use "clean / premium / trustworthy." If you can't pick 3, fall back to the simplest version of the question: **how do you want people to feel when they see your visuals?**

**Why this works:** a one-liner is a portable decision gate. Faced with any reference, font, or layout, you ask "does this match the 3 vibe words?" — a binary check that a vague intent ("make it look good") can never give you. The slots force the brand to name an audience, a result, a method, and a feel — the four things an art-director-grade visual system must serve.

## 4. The 3 Pillars (Function / Emotion / Edge)

The pillars are the **boundaries that stop style-hopping.** Any reference or design that doesn't support a pillar is OUT.

| Pillar | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Function** | What must be clear/valuable — the offer/outcome |
| **Emotion** | What people should feel |
| **Edge** | What makes you distinct/memorable |

**The Edge test is the hard one.** Your Edge must be something *not everyone in your niche could claim*. The instructor's analogy: putting "responsible" on a resume — everyone says it, so it carries no information. Find the thing only *you* can claim.

| Niche claim | Verdict | Why |
|---|---|---|
| "competitive results with AI" | ❌ not an edge | everyone in the niche says it |
| "high quality visuals" | ❌ not an edge | universal, unfalsifiable |
| "splendid visualization" | ❌ not an edge | adjective, not a position |
| "only real-estate-focused" | ✅ edge | a position only some can claim |
| "agency-grade advertising for small & medium brands" | ✅ edge | specific audience + specific promise |

**Why this works:** an Edge that everyone could claim differentiates nothing — it is noise. The pillars together convert "I'll know good design when I see it" into three falsifiable tests (is it clear? does it create the right feeling? is it distinct?). A reference that fails any of the three is removed *before* it can contaminate the moodboard.

## 5. The Meaning Map — the core craft (the durable dictionary)

The Meaning Map is the **semantic dictionary of design.** It turns subjective taste into defensible decisions: every element carries a meta-message, so there is no randomness. The format is constant:

> **Element → Functional job → Meta-message → Emotion it creates**

Examples that anchor the format:

- White space → clarity → premium confidence → calm trust
- Grid → structure → reliability → safety
- High contrast → readability → authority → decisiveness
- Warm tone → human → approachable → belonging
- Chrome/glass texture → innovation → futurism → curiosity

**Why this works:** "colors and shapes are not decoration — they communicate." When you can name the meta-message of every element, design stops being "I like it" and becomes "this element does job X, sends message Y, creates emotion Z." That is what makes the work *defensible to a paying client* and *reproducible across a series*. Color meaning is **contextual** — the strongest signals come from **consistency** (same roles everywhere), **contrast** (readability/hierarchy), and **role clarity** (base vs accent), not from any single "right" color.

### Color roles (avoid "rainbow branding")

Assign roles, not just colors. This is the antidote to a palette that looks busy and cheap:

| Role | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Base neutrals** | Background / breathing room / premium base |
| **Primary accent** | Focus / brand signature |
| **Support accent** | Rare, secondary emphasis |
| **Status colors** | Success / warning / error |

### A) Color families

| Color | Meaning → Emotion | Note |
|---|---|---|
| **Blue** | trust, stability, competence → calm safety, confidence | Authority/reliability/tech/finance; works best with whitespace |
| **Red** | urgency, power, action → energy, intensity | Best as controlled accent (CTA); too much = aggressive |
| **Green** | growth, balance, health → relief, calm | Progress/success; overuse = "generic wellness" |
| **Yellow** | optimism, attention, warmth → joy, curiosity | Accent only; large areas reduce "premium" |
| **Orange** | momentum, friendliness, bold optimism → upbeat confidence | Strong for creators/offers; restrain or it looks "cheap" |
| **Purple** | creativity, mystique, premium imagination → intrigue, aspiration | Premium creative brands; pair with neutrals. **Tone matters: wrong purple = "color of depression"** |
| **Pink** | play, softness, strong personality → warmth, fun | Tone/shade changes everything (premium ↔ too cute) |
| **Black/White** | authority, clarity, premium restraint → calm control, confidence | Best "premium" base; build hierarchy via contrast + spacing |
| **Brown/Beige** | craft, heritage, grounded quality → warm trust, comfort | Artisanal/food/lifestyle; needs strong type to feel modern |
| **Teal/Cyan** | innovation + calm, modern craft → fresh trust, curiosity | "Lab/atelier" energy; avoid neon |
| **Gray** | neutrality, focus, maturity → stability | Base only; too much = cold without a warmth cue |

> Color meaning is contextual and tone-sensitive. The same hue family can read premium or depressive depending on the exact tone — recommended reading: *"The Little Book of Color" / "The Psychology of Color"* by Karen Haller (covers negative meanings and how exact tone changes meaning). Mechanically: opposite colors on the wheel match, adjacent colors match, same-family colors match — random picks don't.

### B) Shape / form language

| Element | → Job | → Meta-message | → Emotion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sharp angles / triangles | direction, emphasis | precision, speed, authority | drive, intensity |
| Rounded corners / circles | softness, approachability | safety, friendliness | comfort, warmth |
| Geometric grids / rectangles | order, structure | reliability, discipline | calm control |
| Organic / fluid shapes | human movement | emotion, creativity | warmth, curiosity |
| 3D volume / depth | hierarchy, richness | modern high production | desire, awe |
| Flat minimal forms | clarity, focus | premium restraint | calm confidence |

As the course frames it: Apple "doesn't beg for trust, it assumes it"; Coca-Cola's familiarity *is* proof.

## 6. Amateur → Premium: the 5 Rules

These are the mechanical rules that convert a finished layout from amateur to premium. They operate at layer 5 — but only *after* layers 1–4 exist.

| # | Rule | The mechanic |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **One main message per slide** | Ask "What is the ONE thing I want people to read first?" Everything else supports it. |
| 2 | **Typography limit** | **Max 2 fonts**, only **2–3 sizes**. Use **weight** (bold/regular) for hierarchy, not more fonts. Structure: Headline (large/bold) → Supporting line (smaller/regular) → Details (smallest/optional). |
| 3 | **Spacing creates luxury** | Increase margins; equal padding; consistent gaps. Fast fix: if it feels amateur, **remove 20–30% of elements and increase spacing**. |
| 4 | **Consistency wins** | Same grid/spacing/styles across slides; build small repeatable elements (badges, footers, labels) once and reuse. |
| 5 | **Contrast & readability** | Cover must be readable in 1 second; use overlays/gradients on images; avoid thin light text on light backgrounds; test by zooming out to phone size. |

**Why this works:** each rule attacks a specific amateur tell. Multiple fonts → visual noise that signals "no system." Tight spacing → "cheap" because luxury brands are defined by how much space they *waste*. Inconsistent spacing across assets is the single #1 tell of an amateur to a trained eye — a pro catches different spacings across images instantly, while uniform spacing reads as professional *even when nothing else changes*. The 1-second / phone-zoom test is the practical quality gate that makes "is this good?" answerable without taste.

### The two typography sub-rules

- **Combine exactly 2 fonts:** one slightly "designy"/accent font + one basic font. Never stack multiple creative fonts.
- **Use weight, not more fonts/sizes, for emphasis.**

## 7. The "Looks Amateur" 7-Fix List

When something "looks amateur," it is almost always the same core set of causes: too many fonts/sizes, weak hierarchy, inconsistent spacing, low contrast, no system rules, too much content per slide. The fix is a fixed checklist — run it top to bottom:

```text
1. Remove 20–30% of elements
2. Increase margins and spacing
3. Reduce fonts to 1–2
4. Make one headline clearly dominant
5. Add contrast behind text (overlay/gradient)
6. Align everything to the same edges
7. Keep colors minimal (2–3 max + neutrals)
```

**Why this works:** "amateur" is not a mysterious aesthetic failure — it is a small, finite set of mechanical defects. Naming them as a 7-step checklist converts a vague "this looks off" into a deterministic repair sequence. Note the asymmetry with the 5 Rules: the Rules *prevent* the defects at design time; the 7-Fix list *removes* them after the fact. Both target the same core set of causes.

### Additional premium micro-moves (from the live redesign)

These compound on the 7 fixes when polishing a specific asset:

- **Highlight ONE word, never the whole sentence** — highlighting everything = highlighting nothing.
- **Don't waste hierarchy on obvious info** (e.g., "2026" — nobody needs to be told the year).
- **Avoid pure white (#fff) and pure black (#000)** — use a hue (graphite / milkish / grayish). Pure white is "dangerous."
- **Big image = premium.** A strong image does half the design work; make it large. For overlay text, always darken via an *overlay layer* (preferred over reducing opacity), never the raw image.
- **Cutting the subject out / breaking the frame** is a premium move.
- **Consistent corner radius** — if rounded in one place, round it the same everywhere; mixing corner styles is "a big mistake."
- **Rule of thirds** for composition; subject ideally faces toward the center/text.
- **Sell positives, not negatives** — say the benefit ("focus on the dream"), not "less interruptions" / "less of X."

## 8. Consistency ≠ Identical (one signature system, many lanes)

The most-misunderstood principle: consistency does **not** mean every output looks the same. It means every output is recognizably *yours*.

> **The trick: keep your identity consistent, not every output identical.**

A brand is **one signature system**; the work runs in **multiple lanes** under it.

**Example lanes** (an AI creator's output):

- Lane A — cinematic storytelling
- Lane B — direct-response clarity
- Lane C — UGC / social-native

**What stays constant across all lanes (the signature system):**

| Constant | Detail |
|---|---|
| Typography logic | headline/body roles, weights, spacing |
| Layout rhythm | margins, grid discipline, hierarchy |
| Proof format | how credibility is shown |
| Brand markers | logo placement, footer, tag style, anchors |
| Color roles | accents may shift by lane, but stay in the same color *family/palette* |

**Why this works:** the fix for "every AI creative I make looks different" is *not* to make them all identical — that kills variety and the algorithm punishes monotony. It is to hold *identity* constant (the table above) while letting *content* vary. This lets an AI creator output endlessly varied work while staying instantly recognizable. The hard constraint is the color one: **same color family/palette only** — never mix neon yellow + pastel pink + dark green.

### Two consistency models for multiple offers

| Model | When | Mechanic | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A — Master Brand** | Recommended for **personal brands** | ONE visual DNA for YOU; offers differ via *messaging*; visual system stays constant; differentiate offers with small tags (accent-color shifts, labels, icons) | Apple, Spotify, Virgin, Google |
| **B — Brand Family** | Truly separate products | Shared backbone (grid, typography logic, tone) + different accents/imagery lane per sub-brand | Nike (one world, many campaigns), Coca-Cola (master brand + sub-lines) |

> "If trust is built through *you*, master brand usually wins."

### Brand anchors

From the DNA legend, create a few **anchors** = recognizable, recurring design elements/figures that instantly say "this is your brand." Reuse them across assets. A handful of deliberate signature elements is what makes a brand identifiable at a glance — design them on purpose, don't let them emerge by accident.

## 9. References: cross-industry beats in-niche

The default mistake is to gather references from inside your own niche. That reproduces the niche's clichés. The pro move is **cross-industry**.

> **References do NOT have to come from your niche** — cross-industry is often where the best direction comes from.

| You want… | Borrow from… |
|---|---|
| Premium | luxury / editorial |
| Trust | finance / tech |
| Energy | sports |
| Cinematic | film posters / A24 campaigns |

**Why this works:** copying within your niche reproduces what everyone in the niche already does — you converge on the cliché. Borrowing the *feel* you want from an unrelated industry imports an under-owned look into a niche that hasn't seen it. The course's concrete example: the AI niche is over-saturated with neon/purple/blue, so going **white** is an under-owned, instantly-differentiating position.

**Scan competitors for exactly two reasons:** (1) avoid clichés / find under-owned gaps, (2) mine references. The white-space example above is precisely a gap found by scanning the saturated default.

> **Moodboard ≠ Pinterest dump (for branding).** A branding moodboard is *evidence*, built in intentional lanes — not a pile of things you liked. Free Pinterest collecting is fine for *training your eye*, but not for branding.

### Moodboard lanes (for branding)

| Lane | Must contain |
|---|---|
| Mood / Emotion | the target feeling |
| Typography vibe | type direction |
| Imagery world | lighting / texture / subjects |
| Graphic language | shapes / frames / materials |
| *(optional)* Proof / Presentation | credibility format |

### Reference 3-lane system + cross-industry (the homework structure)

| Lane | Content | # refs | Validation question |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1 — Hero visuals** | mood, cinematic, impact | 4 | "If I used this as my cover style, would it instantly look premium?" |
| **2 — Clarity layouts** | editorial structure, hierarchy | 4 | "Can I reuse this layout for 3 posts next week without it breaking?" |
| **3 — Proof / Portfolio** | case slides, outcomes, before/after, credibility | 4 | "Does this format reduce risk and make me look credible?" |
| **+ cross-industry** | luxury / editorial / tech / sports / film matching your vibe words | 3 | — |

## 10. ChatGPT as Art Director (the central prompt, verbatim)

Once the Visual DNA Legend exists, hand it to ChatGPT to convert into a finished, defensible visual system. Use this prompt verbatim:

```text
"Here is my Visual DNA Legend. Act as an art director.
1. Turn it into a coherent concept narrative.
2. Propose a complete visual system: color roles, typography direction, layout rules, imagery rules, graphic elements.
3. Explain the meta-message behind each choice.
4. Give 3 variations (A/B/C) that still fit the same positioning.
5. Provide an evaluation checklist so I can stay consistent."
```

**Target:** ChatGPT. **Purpose:** convert the Visual DNA Legend into a finished visual system with A/B/C options and a consistency checklist.

**How to feed it:** upload **everything** you gathered — competitor screenshots, all moodboard lanes, customer feedback. More input = better output. Then iterate in dialog: tighten keywords, remove anything off-positioning, strengthen proof cues. If it drifts, pull it back ("this should be the mood; I want people to feel X").

**Why this works:** the prompt's five numbered asks map exactly onto the pyramid and the Meaning Map. Ask 1 (concept narrative) = layer 3. Ask 2 (visual system) = layer 4. Ask 3 (meta-message per choice) = the Meaning Map applied — it forces the model to *justify* every element, which is what keeps the output defensible rather than decorative. Ask 4 (3 variations within the same positioning) prevents lock-in while preserving consistency. Ask 5 (evaluation checklist) hands you a reusable consistency gate for every future asset. The legend is the input that makes all five asks productive — without it, the model has nothing to be an art director *of*.

### The full pipeline this prompt sits inside

```text
Visual DNA Legend → ChatGPT (art director) → PromptEngine → Nano Banana → Figma assets → consistent templated series
```

Each step inherits the meaning from the one before, so nothing downstream is random. PromptEngine (the course's custom prompt-crafting GPT) takes your visual concept and outputs a clean image-gen prompt — for the moodboard grid first, then each individual element. The image gen (Nano Banana / Higgsfield) executes; Figma assembles and templatizes. (Tool names rotate — the *handoff structure* is the durable part.)

## 11. The Visual DNA Legend — 15-item template

The Legend is the single artifact that captures the whole pyramid. It is the input to the art-director prompt (§10) and the constant alignment check for all downstream work. Write it down even if you "remember it" — flow and excitement make you forget; the written legend is a constant alignment check.

```text
1.  Brand/project NAME + what it is (1 line)
2.  GOAL (what visuals must achieve)
3.  AUDIENCE (who must resonate)
4.  POSITIONING (what you're known for)
5.  CONCEPT statement (one sentence)
6.  3 PILLARS (Function / Emotion / Edge)
7.  KEYWORDS (5–7 vibe words)
8.  ANTI-STYLE list (what we avoid)
9.  COMPETITOR patterns (what's overused + our differentiation)
10. REFERENCE direction (3 inside niche + 3 outside niche)
11. MOODBOARD lanes (what each lane must contain)
12. MEANING MAP (6–10 elements with meta-messages)
13. SYSTEM draft (color roles, typography roles, layout/grid rules, imagery rules, graphic element rules)
14. PROOF plan (how credibility shows visually: portfolio/case format)
15. (Optional) LORE (character, world, symbols, gamification)
```

**Why this works:** the 15 items are the pyramid (layers 1–4) plus their evidence (references, moodboard, meaning map) plus their realization plan (system draft, proof plan) — assembled in the order an art director would build them. Items 1–6 are strategy/positioning/concept; 7–11 are the evidence base; 12–14 are the system; 15 is the high ceiling (lore/world-building creates the "wow effect people come for" — optional but a major level-up for communities, apps, and startups). Filling all 15 is what takes the "~3 hours on the legend"; once it exists, the art-director prompt and every asset are fast because they inherit from it.

## 12. Decision rules / heuristics (do / don't)

A consolidated checklist of the art-direction reflexes. Read top to bottom when shaping or auditing any brand visual.

- **Font/color is the LAST decision, never the first.** Build Visual DNA first; only then choose colors. (When asked "which colors for real estate/construction?" the correct answer is: do the research + Visual DNA first, *then* ask — though "blue/white/a little green" is the basic premium default.)
- **Consistency is built at the TOP of the pyramid, not by making more posts.**
- **Keep identity consistent, not every output identical** — one signature system, many lanes.
- **Edge must be unique** — if most people in your niche could claim it, it's not an edge.
- **Visualize, don't list.** Avoid bullet lists in visuals; replace with metaphors — pyramid for levels, diagram for steps, puzzle for parts, map for modules, flows/roadmaps. The brain is visual; associations are remembered better than text.
- **References do NOT have to come from your niche** — cross-industry imports an under-owned feel.
- **Scan competitors for two reasons:** avoid clichés / find under-owned gaps, and mine references.
- **Inconsistent spacing instantly screams amateur** — same spacing everywhere = professional.
- **Same color FAMILY/palette only.** Never mix neon yellow + pastel pink + dark green. (Color wheel: opposites match, adjacents match, random ones don't.)
- **Combine exactly 2 fonts**; use weight (not more fonts/sizes) for emphasis.
- **Highlight ONE word, never the whole sentence.**
- **Don't waste hierarchy on obvious info.**
- **Avoid pure white (#fff) and pure black (#000)** — use a hue.
- **Big image = premium**; darken with an overlay layer for text, never the raw image.
- **Cutting the subject out / breaking the frame** is a premium move.
- **Consistent corner radius everywhere.**
- **Sell positives, not negatives.**
- **Carousels** are the only acceptable place for a lot of text ("80% won't read it, but they'll look") — a compromise, not for when the message really matters.
- **Always have your legend written down** — flow makes you forget; it's a constant alignment check.
- **Always start any design by looking at competitors/references first** — even cross-niche.
- **You don't need perfection to publish/grow** — a 121k-follower account had "really bad" design. Don't withhold posts waiting for perfect; but still aim for good.
- **AI is a balance, not 100%.** "There still needs to be a human behind it." Basics (color theory, layout, references) + AI speed beats either alone — know the fundamentals or AI output stays amateur.

## 13. The "Visuals that Sell" 1-second test

The final gate before an asset ships. A visual passes if all five hold under a *one-second glance at phone size*:

| Check | Question |
|---|---|
| **Clarity** | What is this? Who is it for? (clear in 1 second) |
| **Hierarchy** | What do I read first? |
| **Trust cues** | Is there restraint, spacing, consistency, calmness? |
| **Proof cues** | Portfolio format, results, case slides, credibility markers? |
| **Direction** | What action do you want? |

> **"Beautiful without clarity is entertainment. Branding needs commercial intention."**

**Why this works:** this test closes the loop back to the thesis (§0). Each check maps to a layer of meaning: Clarity and Hierarchy are the 1-second association test; Trust cues and Proof cues are the Brand Core's line 5 made visible; Direction is the Conversion end of *Meaning → Associations → Trust → Conversion*. An asset that is beautiful but fails Clarity is "entertainment" — it does not sell, because it never built the association the strategy required. This is the practical reason the whole framework exists upstream of the font decision.
