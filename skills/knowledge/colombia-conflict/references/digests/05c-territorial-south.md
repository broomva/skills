# Colombia adentro — Magdalena Medio · Nariño/Sur del Cauca · Orinoquía · Valle/Norte del Cauca — Digest

*Colombia adentro* is the fourteen-book **territorial volume** of *Hay Futuro Si Hay Verdad* (2022), the final report of Colombia's Truth Commission (*Comisión de la Verdad*, CEV). Where the national volumes narrate the war thematically, *Colombia adentro* tells it **from the territories** — letting each region's geography, economy, and peoples explain why the armed conflict took the specific shape it did there. The wager of the volume is that Colombia's war is not one war but a mosaic of regional wars stitched together by a few national logics: the **extractive enclave** (oil, gold, palm, sugar, coca), the **colonization frontier** where state institutions never arrived but armed orders did, and the recurring **alliance of landowners, the military, narco-capital and politicians** that produced counter-insurgent paramilitarism. The four southern/central books digested here — Magdalena Medio, Nariño y Sur del Cauca, Orinoquía, and Valle del Cauca y Norte del Cauca — span the cradle of paramilitarism, the Pacific coca periphery, the petro-paramilitary llanos, and the narco-port-and-sugar corridor. Together they show how *resource + abandonment + capital + complicity = durable armed order*.

## Magdalena Medio — *La cuna del paramilitarismo*

**Dominant actors · trajectory.** The region is the birthplace of organized Colombian guerrilla and of organized paramilitarism alike. The **ELN** was founded here in 1964 (Fabio Vásquez Castaño, Víctor Medina Morón, Ricardo Lara Parada), nearly destroyed by *Operación Anorí* (1973), then rebuilt around oil-infrastructure sabotage. **FARC** expanded after its 1982 Seventh Conference, splitting Frente 4 into Frentes 11 and 23 and adopting the *gramaje* (per-kilo coca tax). The decisive innovation, though, was counter-insurgent: from 1978–1984 cattle ranchers, narcos and Army brigades fused into the first integrated paramilitary model. Key figures — **Ramón Isaza** (the campesino chief of the *Autodefensas Campesinas del Magdalena Medio*, ACMM), **Gonzalo and Henry Pérez** of Puerto Boyacá, the **MAS** (*Muerte a Secuestradores*, 1981) brand, narcos **Pablo Escobar** and **Gonzalo Rodríguez Gacha ("El Mexicano")** who bought up haciendas, and ex-officer **Luis Meneses Báez**. The **Fifth and Fourteenth Brigades** and battalions (Bárbula, Luciano D'Elhuyar, Nueva Granada) supplied training, intelligence and cover — the report records 59 active officers and 104 civilians documented as collaborating. The structure was nationalized by the **Castaño brothers** into the AUC and, after 2000, into the **Bloque Central Bolívar** (BCB), commanded by **Carlos Mario Jiménez "Macaco"** and **Ernesto "Báez" Duque** — the largest paramilitary macrostructure in the country (80+ municipalities, ~31,364 registered victims, 1997–2006).

**Key dynamics.** The war fused an **oil enclave** (Ecopetrol's Barrancabermeja refinery, the *Unión Sindical Obrera* / USO) with an **agrarian land conflict** and an early-1980s cocaine bonanza along the *Troncal de la Paz*. Puerto Boyacá became the administrative-operational template — the **"Puerto Boyacá model"**: clandestine detention/torture centers, *comisarios políticos*, cooptation of *Juntas de Acción Comunal*, electoral conquest (1986), and a standing repertoire of massacre. The war then **urbanized**: between 1998 and 2002 the paramilitaries laid siege to and conquered Barrancabermeja itself.

**Emblematic cases.** The **May 16, 1998 Barrancabermeja massacre/disappearances** (planned with DAS/Army knowledge); **La Rochela** (Jan 1989, 19 judicial investigators killed, ordered by Gacha/Escobar/Carranza/Pérez); **Segovia** (1988 bombings); **Vuelta Acuña** and **La Corcovada** (Cimitarra); the **Llana Caliente** killing during the 1988 *paro del nororiente*; and the murder of *Asociación de Trabajadores Campesinos del Carare* (ATCC) founders. The CNMH counted ~261 massacres in 1982–1996.

**Economic drivers.** Oil (refinery + pipeline fuel theft, which financed up to ~80% of BCB revenue by 2003–05); **cattle ranching** (*ganadería extensiva*); **African palm** (southern Cesar; 70 palm-sector unionists killed 1985–96); **coca** in the south of Bolívar; **gold** in the *Serranía de San Lucas*; and pervasive **land re-concentration** (*despojo*) reversing earlier peasant *titulación*.

**Principal victims.** USO oil unionists (106 killed 1987–2001), palm and cattle-zone workers, campesinos (8,000–10,000 displaced from south Bolívar to Barrancabermeja in 1998 alone), human-rights defenders, *Unión Patriótica* members, and the urban poor of Barrancabermeja's northeastern barrios.

**Distinctive pattern.** Magdalena Medio is the **laboratory exported nationally** — the proof that oil + unresolved agrarian conflict + narco capital + military complicity produces *durable* paramilitarism. Successor structures (Clan Isaza, Gaitanistas, Rastrojos) persist.

## Nariño y Sur del Cauca — *La violencia de la llegada de la guerra*

**Dominant actors · trajectory.** War arrived **late but with extreme intensity**. **FARC** entered via Frente 8 (Sur del Cauca, 1978) and Frente 29 (Nariño cordillera), then built Pacific-facing mobile columns — *Jacobo Arenas*, *Mariscal Sucre*, *Daniel Aldana* — converting the front into a cocaine apparatus. The **ELN** held the Patía cordillera (*Frente Comuneros del Sur*). **Paramilitaries** came only in 2000–2002: the AUC's **Bloque Libertadores del Sur** and a **Bloque Pacífico**, with the **Rastrojos** continuing post-demobilization. In a notable *alianza sui generis* (2006), the ELN and Rastrojos jointly fought FARC. Post-2010 came **Águilas Negras**, GAO splinters, and after 2016 the **Clan del Golfo** allied to the FARC-dissident **Frente Oliver Sinisterra**. The **Army and the Navy/Armada** intensified on the coast (Operations *Tsunami*, *Júpiter II*).

**Key dynamics.** A **Pacific frontier** with Ecuador, historically abandoned by the state, became Colombia's densest **coca corredor**. *Plan Colombia* aerial fumigation (*aspersión aérea* with glyphosate) pushed cultivation south after 1999, making Nariño the top producing department. Displacement exploded from 888 (1991) to 33,361 (2002). The war landed on **ethnic territories** — Afro-Colombian *consejos comunitarios*, and Indigenous **Awá** resguardos, **Eperara Siapidara** and **Pastos**.

**Emblematic cases.** Repeated **Awá massacres**: *Los Uvos* (1990, Army), Altaquer (Aug 2006, Army), the **Tortugaña-Telembí** killings (2008–09) and the **Gran Rosario massacre** (Aug 2009, 12 Awá including six children, by FARC's *Mariscal Sucre*); the 2006 *Júpiter II* bombardment of Awá resguardos. After 2016, the killing of coca-substitution **social leaders** around Tumaco/Alto Mira.

**Economic drivers.** **Coca/cocaine** above all (Tumaco among the most coca-dense municipalities on earth; *cristalizaderos* taxed by armed groups; 56,793 ha by 2017); **illegal gold mining**; **African palm** in Tumaco (10,000+ ha by 1980); the **port of Tumaco** and the **Trasandino oil pipeline** as strategic export/attack nodes.

**Principal victims.** The **Awá people** (eight documented massacres 2006–11; ~480,000 ha of territory under pressure; confinement), Afro-Colombian coastal communities (Guapi, Timbiquí, López de Micay, Olaya Herrera), coca-growing campesinos, social leaders (84 killed in Nariño 2016–20), and **landmine victims** (Nariño the second-most mine-contaminated department).

**Distinctive pattern.** The war as the **violent late arrival of state and market** into an abandoned ethnic-territorial periphery — reconfigured around **drug routes, not ideology** — with the 2016 accord collapsing into a fight over cocaine corridors.

## Orinoquía — *Petro-renta, retaguardia y guerra entre paras*

**Dominant actors · trajectory.** The **FARC** grew from 1950s *autodefensas campesinas*; the **Bloque Oriental** (around Mesetas/La Uribe, led from the 1980s by **"Mono Jojoy"**) became its strongest bloc and **strategic rear guard** (*retaguardia estratégica*). The **ELN** held parts of Arauca (*Frente Domingo Laín*). Paramilitarism ran in generations: early *MAS* and the **Carranceros** of emerald lord **Víctor Carranza**; the **Autodefensas Campesinas del Casanare** (ACC / *Buitragueños*, **Héctor Buitrago "Martín Llanos"**); Rodríguez Gacha's *masetos* (his **La Reforma** training school); then the AUC's **Bloque Centauros** under **Miguel Arroyave** (who bought the "franchise" for ~US$6M). A **paramilitary civil war** between Centauros and the ACC (2002–2004) killed hundreds before Arroyave's own assassination (Sept 2004).

**Key dynamics.** An **oil economy** drove everything: Casanare's *regalías* (royalties) leapt with **Cusiana-Cupiagua** (BP), and paramilitaries skimmed ~10% of municipal budgets and contracts; Arauca's **Caño Limón** field and the **Caño Limón–Coveñas pipeline** (Occidental) became the ELN's prime *objetivo militar*. Layered atop this: a **colonization frontier** with *llanero* cattle culture, a **coca economy** (FARC *gramaje*; the *Tranquilandia* lab complex seized 1984), and the **despeje** demilitarized zone (1998–2002, ~42,000 km²) in which FARC ran a quasi-state *orden social insurgente*.

**Emblematic cases.** The **Mapiripán massacre** (Jul 1997, ~50 killed by ACCU airlifted with documented military complicity); **Puerto Alvira/Caño Jabón** (1998); the Centauros-vs-ACC inter-paramilitary massacres; **Operación Berlín** (a FARC column of which 141 of 363 were minors); the **Unión Patriótica genocide** (*Plan Esmeralda*, Meta+Guaviare = 1,315 cases); and **"falsos positivos"** — Sixth Division/GAULA units in Casanare confessing to passing off innocents as *bajas en combate*.

**Economic drivers.** **Oil royalties** captured by paramilitaries; **coca**; **cattle** *hatos* as laundering vehicles; **land grabbing** feeding **African palm** agro-industry (e.g., Puerto Gaitán's *La Fazenda/Aliar* complex on irregularly adjudicated land).

**Principal victims.** Campesino *colonos* and oil-zone unionists; Indigenous **Sikuani** (hunted in *guahibiadas*), **Jiw**, and nomadic **Nukak**; one of Colombia's highest **forced-disappearance** rates (Casanare/Meta); mine and child-recruitment victims (Orinoquía = ~55% of national minor-recruitment cases). Regional totals (UARIV, to 2019): ~774,000 displaced, ~92,000 killed, ~32,000 disappeared.

**Distinctive pattern.** **Oil-funded war + paramilitaries killing paramilitaries + FARC strategic depth + state facilitation** — the colono frontier transformed into a **petro-paramilitary fiefdom**, with *terra nullius* narratives used to justify agro-industrial seizure.

## Valle del Cauca y Norte del Cauca — *Narco-capital, puerto y caña*

**Dominant actors · trajectory.** The **Cali Cartel** (Rodríguez Orejuela brothers, "Chepe" Santacruz, "Pacho" Herrera) built an "aseptic," business-integrated narco-power; the **Norte del Valle cartel** (Urdinola, **"Rasguño,"** **Diego Montoya "don Diego," Wilber Varela "Jabón"**) was far more savage, pioneering *casas de pitufeo* (chop-houses). The **Rastrojos-vs-Machos** war (2002–07) killed 3,000+. Paramilitarism arrived as the AUC's **Bloque Calima** (formal incursion Jun 1999, La Moralia/Tuluá; commanders "39" then **"HH" Veloza García**; demobilized 2004), with ~49% of its verified crimes involving *Fuerza Pública* participation (Third Brigade officers, police intelligence chief Danilo González). **FARC** built Frente 6 / **Bloque Occidental** in north Cauca and urban militias in Cali's barrios; **ELN**, the **Quintín Lame (MAQL)** Indigenous guerrilla, and **M-19** all operated here. Post-2016 dissident columns (**Jaime Martínez**, **Dagoberto Ramos**) plus **Clan del Golfo** and Mexican cartels (Sinaloa, CJNG) reconfigured the war.

**Key dynamics.** **Cali** as narco-capital and site of urban war and *limpieza social* death squads; **Buenaventura** — Colombia's main Pacific **port** — as a zone of hyper-violence and chop-house disappearance, controlled post-2016 by **La Empresa / Los Urabeños / La Local**; the flat-valley **sugarcane** (*caña*) agro-industrial corridor (~30 *ingenios*; cane area 61,600 ha in 1960 → ~201,000 ha by 2009) with its racialized *corte de caña* labor regime and crushed strikes (Riopaila 1975–76; 2005, 2008); the **Naya** corridor linking interior to coast; and the densely organized **Afro/Indigenous Cauca** (CRIC, ACIN, ACONC, *consejos comunitarios*, the unarmed **Guardia Indígena**).

**Emblematic cases.** The **Naya massacre** (April 2001, Bloque Calima sweeping the Alto Naya, mass exodus of Afro and Indigenous communities to Buenaventura); the **Trujillo massacre** (1988–94, chainsaw killings, Father **Tiberio Fernández**); **El Nilo** (Dec 1991, 20 Nasa killed over land recovery, triggering Incora's 15,663-ha reparation pact); the **La María** church kidnapping (1999, 194 by ELN) and the **km 18** kidnappings (2000); the **twelve Valle deputies** (kidnapped 2002, eleven killed 2007 by FARC); Buenaventura's chop-house disappearances; and 13 post-2016 north-Cauca massacres (51 dead).

**Economic drivers.** **Narco-trafficking** through the **port of Buenaventura** and the Pacific; **sugarcane/caña** and the *ingenios* (≈286,000 jobs; a top foreign-exchange crop); **cocaine** processing and **coca** in the cordillera; a 21st-century **marihuana** *boom* (high-THC "creepy," concentrated in Toribío/Corinto/Caloto); and **illegal gold mining** displacing Afro artisanal *barequeo* (the 2010 *La Toma* Constitutional Court ruling on *consulta previa*).

**Principal victims.** **Afro-Colombian** communities of Buenaventura and the north-Cauca councils; **Indigenous Nasa/Páez** (land massacres, post-2016 forced recruitment — 70% girls in Toribío); the **urban poor** of Cali's *Aguablanca* district; sugarcane **corteros**, trade unionists, social and Indigenous leaders; and the disappeared.

**Distinctive pattern.** A **fusion of narco-capital + paramilitarism + agro-industrial/port economy**, expressed as **urban war** in Cali/Buenaventura and as a **racialized geography** in which Afro and Indigenous peoples are targeted for dispossession — answered by an unusually strong tradition of **ethnic civil resistance** (Guardia Indígena, *mingas*, *consejos comunitarios*).

## Cross-region synthesis + coined concepts

Across the four books one engine recurs: **resource + abandonment + capital + complicity**. Each region's defining commodity selected its war — **oil** (Magdalena Medio, Casanare/Arauca), **coca and the port/route** (Nariño, Buenaventura), **sugar and gold** (Valle/Cauca), **cattle and palm and royalties** (Orinoquía). In every case the state was present as **complicity, not protection**: brigades that trained or transported paramilitaries (Magdalena Medio's Fifth/Fourteenth, Calima's Third Brigade), royalty and adjudication machinery captured for *despojo* (Orinoquía), and fumigation/militarization standing in for institutions (Nariño). Paramilitarism's **Puerto Boyacá model** — invented in Magdalena Medio — was the franchise replicated everywhere, even turning on itself in the Orinoquía's paras-killing-paras war. And the war's burden fell hardest on the **racialized and unionized**: Afro and Indigenous peoples on the Pacific and in Cauca, oil and sugar workers, *colonos* on the frontier.

**Coined / load-bearing terms:** *cuna del paramilitarismo* (cradle of paramilitarism — Magdalena Medio); the *Puerto Boyacá model*; *gramaje* (per-kilo coca tax); *despojo* (violent dispossession/land re-concentration); *autodefensas* → AUC → *bloques* (Central Bolívar, Centauros, Libertadores del Sur, Calima, Pacífico); *retaguardia estratégica* (FARC strategic rear, Orinoquía); *orden social insurgente* (insurgent social order in the *despeje*); *regalías* / *petro-renta* (oil royalties captured by armed actors); *falsos positivos* (extrajudicial executions passed off as combat kills); *la violencia de la llegada de la guerra* (the violence of the war's arrival — Nariño); *casas de pitufeo* (chop-houses, Buenaventura/Norte del Valle); *corte de caña / corteros* (cane-cutting labor regime); *pescas milagrosas* (roadside mass kidnappings); *consejos comunitarios* and *resguardos* (Afro/Indigenous collective territories); and the *Guardia Indígena* and *mingas* as the volume's emblem of unarmed territorial resistance.
