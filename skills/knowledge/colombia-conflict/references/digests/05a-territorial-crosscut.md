# Colombia adentro — Cross-cutting + Amazonía + Pacífico — Digest

*Digest of the introductory essay, two thematic books (urban war dynamics; the peasantry), and two regional books (Amazonía, Pacífico) of "Colombia adentro," the 14-book territorial volume of the Colombian Truth Commission (CEV) final report* Hay Futuro Si Hay Verdad *(2022). Coined Spanish terms are preserved with glosses.*

## 1. Identity & purpose of the territorial volume

*Colombia adentro: relatos territoriales sobre el conflicto armado* is the CEV's territorial tome — 14 books that narrate the sixty-year internal armed conflict not from Bogotá outward but from the territories inward. Its method, following Alfredo Molano, is to show *el revés de la historia de la guerra* ("the reverse of war's history"): the war as lived by "the most victimized, excluded and silenced sectors and territories." The volume's stated purpose is to map, region by region, the human-rights and IHL violations suffered by each prioritized territory, the *territorial outcomes* of the war, the populations and political projects affected, and — crucially — "the interests of those who benefited." It treats *territorio* not as inert space but as "a process constructed through social relationships, expressions of identity, and shared purpose": *"el territorio es el lugar por el que camina la vida"* ("territory is the place by which life walks"). The volume combines cross-cutting thematic books (including the two digested here — urban dynamics and the peasantry) with region-specific books (Amazonía and Pacífico among them).

## 2. The territorial thesis (intro essay)

The intro's foundational claim is geographic: *"El problema del territorio para Colombia es capital y el conflicto armado colombiano no se puede pensar si se sustrae al mapa de su territorio"* ("the question of territory is capital for Colombia, and the Colombian armed conflict cannot be thought if subtracted from the map of its territory"). War did not blanket the country uniformly; "the presence of armed confrontation has been territorially differentiated and transformed over time, according to the style and level of regional integration into national development dynamics."

The organizing concept is **territorial duality** (*dualidad territorial del Estado*) and what Margarita Serje calls *el revés de la nación* ("the reverse of the nation"): peripheral regions and "internal frontiers" — long imagined as wild, empty *baldíos* ("lands of no one"), as obstacles to integration — became "the negative, the reverse of national space." Entire regions were assigned subordinate places in a hierarchical geographic order, "converting entire regions into marginal or peripheral spaces" and excluding their inhabitants from "democratic processes and wealth and wellbeing production." This is the spatial logic captured across the volume by the phrase **"más territorio que Estado"** ("more territory than State") — formal sovereignty thin, security-focused and counterinsurgent; private and illegal capital filling the void.

War concentrated in two kinds of space: zones of colonization along the *frontera agraria* (agricultural frontier) and territories of extractive expansion — mining, oil, timber, cattle, coca — where the State's presence was weak and militarized. There, confrontation entangled with "economic interests to control strategic lands for extensive agricultural or livestock exploitation, for extractive projects, legalization of illegal capital, or control of narcotrafficking corridors."

## 3. Cross-cutting findings

**Center–periphery and State "absence."** The structural root is a colonial-inherited territorial model that privileges private capital over public provision. Distant regions (Orinoquía, Amazonía, the Pacific, the northeast frontier) "did not reach population levels to generate incentives for political elites to provide public goods," leaving inhabitants in "precarity of state presence." The CEV nuances the victims' word *ausencia del Estado* (State absence): the State was not absent but *selectively present* — present as security force, absent as guarantor of rights — a *dualidad territorial*. Decentralization (1991 Constitution) and economic liberalization, meant to democratize, instead "transferred conflict to disputes over local power" captured by regional elites through *clientelismo armado* (armed clientelism).

**The land question.** Beneath the war lies an unresolved conflict over land property running from colonial *mercedes de tierra* through the hacienda system to the failed agrarian reforms of 1961. The *Pacto de Chicoral* (1972) "definitively buried the idea of modifying property distribution"; only 5.4% of state-acquired land ever came through expropriation. By the 1980s the model collapsed into *contrarreforma agraria* (counter-agrarian reform).

**Reprimarization and war financing.** Colombia's integration into global markets produced *reprimarización* (reprimarization) of the economy: coffee fell from ~50% of exports (1985) to ~8% (2000); oil, minerals (gold, nickel, coal), and coca replaced it. In the 1990s, "the abandonment of peasant economies, the bankruptcy of producers via import liberalization, and growing rural unemployment coincided spatially and temporally with the growth of armed groups and illicit crops." Coca became "the only lifeline for thousands"; FARC taxed and regulated it, and its deepening narco-involvement transformed "the relationship between ends and means."

**Armed actors as territorial orders.** Guerrillas (FARC, ELN, EPL) inserted into colonization zones via *órdenes insurgentes* (insurgent orders) — providing security, regulating land disputes, imposing conduct codes, filling the State vacuum, while also imposing "silence and submission of bodies and territory." Paramilitarism arose as "private provision of coercion and security," sustained by four agent types: legal rural elites (ranchers), illegal elites (narcos), regional politico-economic elites, and State security forces. The State appears throughout in *connivencia* (complicity).

**Scale.** More than nine million victims, more than nine million displaced, and more than eight million hectares usurped or abandoned — concentrated in the peripheral territories that already bore "intolerable levels of inequality and poverty."

## 4. Dinámicas Urbanas de la Guerra — key findings

The book breaks the rural-war myth, establishing a **bidirectional relationship between urbanization and conflict** (*urbanización de la guerra*): the war shaped cities even as cities became theaters of war. Over 87% of war-displaced rural people fled to cities, creating *ciudades receptoras* (receiver cities) and informal peripheral settlements; by 2002–2016 cities held over half of all registered victims, led by Buenaventura, Tumaco, and Medellín.

**Actors:** *milicias urbanas* (urban militias) of ELN, FARC, and M-19, and dedicated fronts (FARC's Jacobo Arenas in Medellín, Antonio Nariño in Bogotá, Manuel Cepeda in Cali); paramilitaries with deep police *connivencia* (especially Medellín's *oficinas*); and State forces (police, army, the DAS intelligence agency), which ran large operations and committed extrajudicial executions.

**Dynamics:** *desplazamiento intraurbano* (intra-urban forced displacement) — residents fleeing neighborhood to neighborhood, largely unregistered; *fronteras invisibles* (invisible borders) partitioning comunas (Comuna 13 in Medellín, Aguablanca in Cali, Ciudad Bolívar in Bogotá) under armed checkpoints; *limpieza social* (social cleansing) — extrajudicial killing of youth, LGBTIQ+ people, street vendors, and "undesirables" under an anti-crime guise; and armed control of informal economies (transport, vending, curfews).

**Victims and invisibilization:** youth in marginal barrios, displaced families, social leaders, students, unionists, kidnap victims. Their suffering was rendered structurally invisible because cities were not framed as war zones — urban violence was coded as "criminal," not political, so victims fell outside reparations. As the book puts it, cities "produce the greatest number of violent deaths and yet they are not discussed when addressing armed conflict or the purposes of peace."

## 5. El Campesinado y la Guerra — key findings

**The peasantry as principal victim.** Of 10.6 million victimizing acts in the Unique Victims Registry (RUV), 4.8 million (45.5%) struck the *campesinado*; peasants are 45.8% of the 8.77 million forcibly displaced. The CEV defines the peasantry as an *sujeto intercultural* (intercultural subject) — roughly 28.4% of the population (12.9 million in 2020).

**The peasantry as political subject.** The deeper wound is the *negación del campesinado* — the erasure of the peasant as a *sujeto político de derechos* (political subject of rights). From the 1926 untitled-land ruling and Ley 200 of 1936 (which legitimized *latifundista* hoarding via *presunción de propiedad*, a presumption favoring large holders), peasants were treated as legal objects, not subjects — and from the 1960s as suspects, their organizing criminalized as subversion (the 1978 *Estatuto de Seguridad*) and stigmatized as guerrilla-aligned.

**Land and the frontier.** Reforms relegated peasants to the *frontera agrícola* (agricultural frontier) rather than redistributing fertile central-valley land — a strategy to preserve elite property. La Violencia dispossessed ~300,000 parcels; ~2 million hectares are now in restitution. By the 1980s the system inverted into *contrarreforma agraria*: narco-traffickers amassed ~4 million hectares (≈48% of the most productive land) with State complicity.

**Actors against the peasantry:** guerrillas controlled peasant territory coercively (forced recruitment, *órdenes sociales*, anti-personnel mines, coca taxation); paramilitaries and narcos ran the *contrarreforma agraria*, assassinating leaders (Fensuagro: 572 affiliates, 1987–2020) and decimating the Unión Patriótica (~3,000 killed); State forces criminalized organizing and sprayed *glifosato* (glyphosate); agro-industrial and extractive capital pushed monocrops and wage dependence.

**Recognition demanded:** the book affirms *zonas de reserva campesina* (peasant reserve zones, Ley 160/1994) as spaces of territorial autonomy — stigmatized by Uribe as *emporios del terrorismo* — and recommends adopting the 2018 UN Declaration on the Rights of Peasants, on which Colombia abstained. Its conclusion: *"El campesinado fue la principal víctima del conflicto armado interno… Un efecto de la guerra es el desconocimiento del campesinado como sujeto político, cultural y de derechos."*

## 6. Amazonía — actors, dynamics, victims, resource economies

**Thesis:** the Amazon (Putumayo, Caquetá, Guaviare, Guainía, Vaupés, Amazonas — a third of the national territory) is the archetypal *frontera de colonización* (colonization frontier) where *más territorio que Estado* applies literally. Three extractive waves structured its violence: rubber (*caucho*) c.1890–1930 — the *cautchouc*, *"la madera que llora"* ("the tree that weeps"), enslaving ~50,000 Indigenous people through *endeude* (debt-servitude); oil (Putumayo, from the 1940s–50s); and coca (1970s–2016), which *"reemplazaba la producción de alimentos de pancoger"* (replaced subsistence food crops). State-sponsored colonization (Ley 135/1961, slogan *"Tierras sin hombre para hombres sin tierra"* — "land without men for men without land") erased Indigenous presence and preceded armed control.

**Actors:** FARC's *Bloque Sur* dominated from the early 1980s (fronts 14, 15, 49 in Caquetá; 32 in Putumayo; 1st in Vaupés; 16 in Guainía), exercising *para-Estado* (para-statehood) — courts, taxes, *manuales de convivencia* (conduct codes). The *Toma de Mitú* (Nov 1998) marked FARC's military peak. Paramilitaries (Bloque Sur Putumayo, 1998–2006) entered to protect narcos, killing ≥2,500 civilians (massacres at El Tigre, El Placer, La Dorada) and running torture schools at Puerto Torres. State forces deployed Plan Colombia and Operación Gato Negro, with monthly *nóminas paralelas* (parallel payrolls) to paramilitaries, and prosecuted aerial fumigation (*aspersión aérea* with glyphosate) that destroyed legal crops and harmed health.

**Resource economies:** coca (raspachines, the 1996 *marchas cocaleras* of ~140,000 peasants, the largely unhonored *Pacto de Orito*), oil, and extensive cattle ranching (hacienda Larandia) driving deforestation and *latifundio* over *colonos*.

**Victims and dynamics:** 271,994 displaced across six departments in 2002–2005 (Operación Tánatos ended the Caguán zone); 14 Indigenous peoples at *"riesgo inminente de exterminio físico y cultural"* (Awá, Embera, Nasa, Sikuani, Murui-muina, Siona, Kofán, Inga, Korebajú, Jiw, Nükak, and others); mercury poisoning from mining; sexual violence and forced contraception by armed groups; LGBTIQ+ persecution under FARC conduct codes; and environmental devastation of the *ríos voladores* ("flying rivers") forest. Indigenous resistance organized (e.g., Umiyac, 1999).

## 7. Pacífico — actors, dynamics, victims, resource economies

**Thesis:** the Pacific — Afro-Colombian and Indigenous majority, ~63% under collective titles (Ley 70/1993) — is a region of extreme biodiversity and radical State absence, where *más territorio que Estado* meets *racismo estructural* (structural racism). Andean elites long coded it as *salvaje* and *baldío*, licensing successive resource plunder. Violence intensified *after* the 1991 ethnic-territorial recognition, as armed actors moved to reassert capitalist control; the region's deepwater ports made it a strategic cocaine-export corridor — *"una guerra por las rentas"* (a war over rents).

**Actors:** FARC fronts (5, 8, 30, 34, 57, 60) consolidated as "occupation armies"; ELN entered via Nariño and the San Juan; the short-lived Indigenous FARIP emerged then disbanded. Paramilitaries deployed massively — Bloque Calima (Buenaventura/Valle, 19 massacres), Bloque Elmer Cárdenas (Chocó/Urabá), Bloque Pacífico, Bloque Libertadores del Sur (Tumaco/Nariño) — financed by merchants, shipping firms, palm planters, and narcos, with documented State-force *connivencia* (weapons, intelligence, safe passage). Post-2005 successor groups (AGC/Gaitanistas, dissidents) persist.

**Resource economies:** colonial gold/platinum mining (enslaving ~15,000 Africans) giving way to artisanal *mazamorreo* and *pancoger* subsistence; industrial timber (*madera*) consuming ~800,000 hectares; African palm (*palma aceitera*) monocultures in Tumaco/Curvaradó/Jiguamiandó, often planted on dispossessed collective land as a development cover for money-laundering; mechanized illegal gold mining (*minería mecanizada*) contaminating rivers; coca laboratories inside *consejos comunitarios*; and port economies (Buenaventura's 1993 privatization cut port jobs, feeding the *casas de pique* — paramilitary torture-houses).

**Victims and dynamics:** 61,710 Afro-descendant and Indigenous people displaced 1996–2005 — among the country's highest rates. Emblematic massacres: **Bojayá** (1999, FARC, 79 dead in a church), **Naya** (2001, Bloque Calima, ~40 killed with machetes), **Sabaletas**, and **Cacarica** (Operación Génesis, 1997, ~12,000 displaced). *Confinamiento* (confinement) barred communities from fishing, hunting, gathering, and ceremony; ethnic-territorial leaders (Afro and Indigenous — Embera, Wounaan, Eperara Siapidara, Awá) were assassinated to break self-determination. The book underscores that violence struck *precisely as* collective titling advanced — the conflict was waged against Ley 70 rights themselves.

## 9. Distinctive insights & coined concepts

- **Más territorio que Estado** ("more territory than State") — the volume's central spatial thesis: thin, security-only sovereignty over vast peripheries that private and illegal capital colonize.
- **Dualidad territorial** / *el revés de la nación* — the persistent split between an integrated, developed core and abandoned extractive peripheries imagined as the "negative" of the nation (Serje).
- **Frontera agraria / frontera de colonización / frontera extractiva** — the war concentrates where the colonization and extraction frontiers advance; the State channels peasants there rather than redistributing fertile land.
- **Reprimarización** — re-primarized export economy (oil, minerals, coca) replacing coffee, temporally coincident with armed-group and illicit-crop growth.
- **Órdenes insurgentes / para-Estado** — guerrilla governance that substitutes for the State (justice, taxes, *manuales de convivencia*) while subjecting bodies and territory.
- **Contrarreforma agraria** — the violent reversal of agrarian reform via *despojo masivo*, leader assassination, and narco land accumulation (≈48% of the best land).
- **Negación del campesinado / sujeto político** — the erasure of the peasantry as a rights-bearing political subject is itself a finding, not just a context.
- **Urbanización de la guerra**, with **desplazamiento intraurbano**, **fronteras invisibles**, **ciudades receptoras**, and **limpieza social** — the city is a battlefield, not a refuge.
- **Confinamiento**, **racismo estructural**, **casas de pique**, and *víctimas del desarrollo* — the Pacific reframes the war as racial-capitalist dispossession in which armed actors are instruments of extractive interests, and violence peaks exactly as ethnic territorial rights are recognized.
- **Connivencia / nóminas paralelas** — recurring evidence of State-force complicity with paramilitarism across regions.

The cross-cutting throughline: the war is legible only as geography. Its map is the map of the *frontera* and the extractive enclave; its principal victim is the *campesinado* and the ethnic-territorial peoples of the peripheries; and its deepest mechanism is a *dualidad territorial* in which the State is present as coercion and absent as right — *más territorio que Estado*.
